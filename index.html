Weather Patterns Across Australia's Capital Cities and Their Potential Impact on Energy Consumption




Singapore's tropical climate stays consistent year-round, with temperatures around 28°C, creating a uniform weather experience. However, Australia offers a completely different story.

Australia’s vast landscape offers a diverse climate, with cities experiencing drastically different weather simultaneously.

While Darwin enjoys tropical heat, cold winds from the Antarctic sweep through Hobart, just 500 kilometers away. This contrast underscores the unique and varied climates across the country.

In this visualization, we explore the weather patterns of Australia’s major cities, revealing the diversity that makes its climate truly distinctive. We will also examine how these weather patterns may correlate with energy usage across the country.

So, what can we learn from analyzing these weather patterns? By connecting what we've observed across temperature, humidity, and rainfall, we can piece together how these factors collectively define the climate of each city.

To determine the climate type of each major Australian city, we analyzed rainfall, temperature, and humidity data, comparing it to the Köppen classification:

Rainfall: Darwin, with the highest rainfall, fits its tropical monsoon (Am) climate. Sydney and Brisbane’s high rainfall aligns with their humid subtropical (Cfa) climates, while Adelaide and Hobart’s lower rainfall matches their semi-arid (BSh) and oceanic (Cfb) climates.

Temperature: Darwin is the hottest, typical of tropical climates. Brisbane, Perth, and Adelaide’s warmth fits their subtropical or Mediterranean climates, while cooler temperatures in Hobart and Melbourne reflect their temperate oceanic climates.

Humidity: Darwin and Sydney’s high humidity supports their tropical and humid subtropical climates, while moderate humidity in Adelaide and Perth indicates semi-arid or Mediterranean climates.

Together, these factors classify the cities from tropical (Darwin) to Oceanic (Hobart, Melbourne, Canberra), with subtropical (Sydney, Brisbane) and Mediterranean (Perth, Adelaide) in between.

Section 1.5) Potential Constraints In Section 2

Before analyzing the correlation between climate type and energy usage, it’s important to acknowledge a limitation: city-specific energy data is unavailable, so state-wide energy data is being used instead. While this may slightly reduce the precision of the analysis, the impact is minimal because...



As shown in the chart, each capital city represents a significant portion of its state's population. In many cases, such as Western Australia, over 80% of the population resides in the capital city. On the other hand, Hobart, which has the smallest proportion, still makes up around 55%. This demonstrates that capital cities typically dominate their respective states in terms of population.

Thus, while relying on state-level data for energy consumption may slightly limit the accuracy of the analysis, it remains highly representative since most of each state's population lives in the capital city. Thus, energy patterns in capital cities are likely to reflect the broader state consumption trends.
