Weather Patterns Across Australia's Capital Cities and Their Potential Impact on Resource Consumption




Singapore's tropical climate stays consistent year-round, with temperatures around 28°C, creating a uniform weather experience. However, Australia offers a completely different story.

Australia’s vast landscape offers a diverse climate, with cities experiencing drastically different weather simultaneously.

While Darwin enjoys tropical heat, cold winds from the Antarctic sweep through Hobart, just 500 kilometers away. This contrast underscores the unique and varied climates across the country.

In this visualization, we explore the weather patterns of Australia’s major cities, revealing the diversity that makes its climate truly distinctive. We will also examine how these weather patterns may correlate with resource consumption (e.g water usage, electrictiy usage) across the country.

So, what can we learn from analyzing these weather patterns? By connecting what we've observed across temperature, humidity, and rainfall, we can piece together how these factors collectively define the climate of each city.

To determine the climate type of each major Australian city, we analyzed rainfall, temperature, and humidity data, comparing it to the Köppen classification:

Rainfall: Darwin, with the highest rainfall, fits its tropical monsoon (Am) climate. Sydney and Brisbane’s high rainfall aligns with their humid subtropical (Cfa) climates, while Adelaide and Hobart’s lower rainfall matches their semi-arid (BSh) and oceanic (Cfb) climates.

Temperature: Darwin is the hottest, typical of tropical climates. Brisbane, Perth, and Adelaide’s warmth fits their subtropical or Mediterranean climates, while cooler temperatures in Hobart and Melbourne reflect their temperate oceanic climates.

Humidity: Darwin and Sydney’s high humidity supports their tropical and humid subtropical climates, while moderate humidity in Adelaide and Perth indicates semi-arid or Mediterranean climates.

Together, these factors classify the cities from tropical (Darwin) to Oceanic (Hobart, Melbourne, Canberra), with subtropical (Sydney, Brisbane) and Mediterranean (Perth, Adelaide) in between.

Section 2) Correlation Between Resource Consumption and Climate Type Across Australian States




In this section, we explore the correlation between resource consumption and climate type across various Australian states. By examining electricity, gas, and water consumption on a per capita basis, we aim to uncover how regional climate characteristics might influence resource usage. The charts that follow reveal trends and potential correlations between these variables, offering insights into how different climates could impact consumption patterns in each state.



The chart shows that the Northern Territory has the highest electricity consumption per capita, likely due to its tropical climate in Darwin, where the hot, humid weather drives higher demand for cooling system like air conditioning. This suggests a possible correlation between climate and energy usage in tropical regions.

For other climate types, the influence seems less pronounced. Western Australia , with a Mediterranean climate, has the second highest consumption, while South Australia , with the same climate, has the lowest, indicating that climate may not be the main factor. States with oceanic and subtropical climates— New South Wales , Queensland , and Victoria —show stable, mid-tier electricity consumption, suggesting that climate may not have a strong influence in these regions.

Section 3) Conclusion




Australia's major cities span various climate types, from the subtropical climates of Sydney and Brisbane to the tropical climate of Darwin. Hobart, Melbourne, and Canberra fall under oceanic climates, while Perth and Adelaide experience a Mediterranean climate.

The analysis shows that tropical and Mediterranean climates have potential correlations with higher gas consumption, while oceanic and subtropical climates exhibit lower usage. Electricity consumption appears less influenced by climate type, with only weak patterns emerging.
